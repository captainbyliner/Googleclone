Windsor has lost 30,000 youth over the past decade. Those who remain face bleak prospects in exchange for the comforts of home.

'Slap in the face'

$0.02. The bank account was empty. It was the cost of a choice Mike Taggart would quickly regret -- deciding to go to college to get a better job for himself and for his family.
Taggart had just been laid off and was relying on the few thousand dollars he had saved to carry him through until he found more work. But one unpaid OSAP loan took it all away.
"When they pulled all that money out, it was like a slap in the face," he says.
"I've got to buy milk for my baby and you've left me with two cents."

'It's sad'

Nick McGuin didn’t get to see his son take his first steps.
He missed out on his second birthday.
He wasn’t home for his anniversary this year.
McGuin is working as a swamper in Fort McMurray -- far from his wife and son in Windsor, but the right place for opportunity.
“It's sad,” he says. “One of the biggest things is leaving your family and friends.
“You don't want to make that move but it's something that we know that we kind of have to.”

The stories are different, but for many youth in Windsor, it comes down to the same thing: a choice to fight for a future here, or to flee toward cities that offer more.
Youth in Windsor are finding themselves overwhelmed with debt, unable to find a job, and forced to choose between staying in their hometown settling for part-time or temporary work, or moving to an area of opportunity but far from family and friends.
Windsor has some of the highest debt default rates in the province.
Fifty per cent of students who took out an OSAP loan had to either refinance it, or stopped paying it altogether.

"High default rates reflect both the high cost of education and also circumstances beyond a students' control,"said Alastair Woods, chairman for the Canadian Federation of Students in Ontario.
“They can’t help that there are six unemployed people for every job vacancy. It’s certainly not their fault that the jobs that they can find aren’t paying the bills.”
Experts say that a disconnect between post-secondary education programs and the job market, and a lack of preparation to take on the debt that comes with training, is leaving young adults without the chance to take risks on good jobs or entrepreneurship opportunities.
"It’s really concerning me that we launch young people with such a large burden right out of school,” said Wendy Dupuis, executive director of the Financial Fitness Centre.
She counsels people struggling with high debt loads and contemplating bankruptcy. She says at least 1 in 10 of the people she sees have some student debt weighing them down.
When students graduate with high debt levels from OSAP, they have six months until they have to start paying the loan back -- in some cases the repayment is as much as $500 a month.” That can leave people with a choice: struggle to make ends meet while paying it off, or find a place where there are better jobs.
